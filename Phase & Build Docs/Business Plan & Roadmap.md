## 1. Executive Summary

AI has commoditized the ability to *write* software. Anyone can ask an AI to generate a CRM, a portal, or a dashboard in minutes. What AI cannot do — and what remains scarce — is wiring that generated software into the messy, regulated, stateful real world: payment processors, accounting systems, compliance requirements, churn behavior, and the thousand small integration failures that happen at 2am.

**Baseplate OS** is infrastructure for that gap. We are strictly B2B, strictly "boring," and strictly focused on the plumbing layer that AI-generated apps need but cannot build themselves.

We do not compete on app generation (a race to zero, dominated by foundation model labs and tools like Cursor/Lovable/v0). We compete on **integration reliability, data continuity, and operational trust** — things that compound over time and cannot be regenerated by a prompt.

The path to building this is a **Vertical Wedge Strategy**: prove the entire stack (Scaffold → Connect → Intelligence) in one narrow, high-friction B2B niche before generalizing. 

**But the real product is the MODULE LIBRARY.** Med Spas is the first vertical to validate + build reusable modules.

**The wedge: Med Spas / Wellness Clinics**
- **Vertical chosen through Phase 0 validation:** 3+ clinic owners confirmed acute pain points
- **12 pain points identified:** 5 from clinic owner calls + 7 from market research (43+ sources)
- **All pain points mapped to Scaffold modules:** Clear roadmap for Phases 1-4
- **Pilot leads identified:** 3 clinic owners ready to test in Phase 5

**The module library (PRIMARY DELIVERABLE):**
- **Phase 1:** Build 16+ reusable modules in `packages/` while solving med spa pain points
- **Phase 2:** Generalize modules into platform layer (Connect API, middleware)
- **Phase 3:** Add intelligence (ML, predictions) + prove modules work across 2nd vertical
- **Phase 4:** Open-source launch + distribute modules as SDK for AI agents

## 2. Core Philosophy

> AI can write code, but it cannot wire the real world.

AI can build a CRM dashboard in 5 minutes, but it cannot seamlessly connect it to Stripe, handle a failed-payment webhook correctly, reconcile a QuickBooks ledger, satisfy a HIPAA audit requirement, or predict which client is about to churn based on six months of behavioral data.

We do not build AI apps. We build the **picks, shovels, and electricity** that make AI-built apps function in the real world.

**Non-negotiable constraints:**
- **Strictly B2B.** No consumer apps, no social, no games. B2B buyers pay for things that save them time or make them money — full stop.
- **Boring is profitable.** The less "exciting" the problem (webhook retries, ledger reconciliation, audit logs), the less competition and the higher the switching cost.
- **Infrastructure, not application.** We win when *other people's* apps depend on us, not when our app wins on features.

## 3. The 3-Layer Product Suite

### Layer 1: Scaffold (The Frameworks)
Open-source, modular B2B templates (CRMs, client portals, invoicing systems, booking systems) that a developer — or an AI coding agent — can use to instantly scaffold a working app for a specific vertical.

- **Purpose:** Top-of-funnel and distribution. This is what gets found on GitHub, gets starred, gets used by solo devs and AI agents alike.
- **Form:** A repo per vertical (e.g., `baseplate-agency-portal`, `baseplate-medspa-crm`), each built on a shared modular core (`baseplate-core`: auth, billing primitives, RBAC, audit logging).
- **Monetization:** None directly. This layer exists to seed Layer 2 adoption. Every Scaffold template ships pre-wired to call Connect by default.

### Layer 2: Connect (The Middleware) — *The Real Business*
A unified API that abstracts the integrations every B2B app eventually needs, so developers (and AI agents) call one consistent interface instead of learning Stripe's API, then QuickBooks', then Twilio's, then SendGrid's, etc.

- **Purpose:** Recurring revenue + the core of the moat. Once a Scaffold app is wired to `api.baseplate.dev/*`, ripping it out means re-implementing every integration from scratch.
- **Form:** REST/webhook API with endpoints like:
  - `POST /v1/payments/invoice` → creates a Stripe invoice, handles retries/dunning
  - `POST /v1/payments/webhook-relay` → normalizes Stripe/PayPal/Square webhooks into one event schema
  - `POST /v1/notify/email` → sends via SendGrid/Postmark with unified templates
  - `POST /v1/accounting/sync` → pushes invoice/payment data to QuickBooks/Xero
  - `POST /v1/auth/session` → magic-link/SSO auth primitives
- **Critical design rule:** **Build only the endpoints your current pilot users actually need.** Do not build a generic "Zapier for AI SaaS" speculatively — that is a multi-year project that will starve the business of revenue before it has any users. Endpoint #1 should exist because a real pilot customer needed it this week.

### Layer 3: Intelligence (The Data Moat)
Premium predictive APIs built on the data flowing through Connect.

- **Purpose:** High-margin, near-zero marginal cost revenue once data volume exists.
- **Form (early, Phase 3):** Not true ML. Start with **rules-based risk flags** — e.g., "3+ failed payments in 90 days," "no login in 30 days," "support ticket volume spike" — packaged as a simple scoring API. This is honest, defensible, and ships in days, not months.
- **Form (later, Phase 4+):** Once enough accounts (target: 50-100+ active accounts with 6+ months of data) exist, layer in actual ML-based churn/LTV prediction. Until that volume exists, do not market this as "AI-powered prediction" — market it as "automated risk flagging," which is true and still valuable.

## 5. The Defensibility (The Moat)

The front-end is commoditized — anyone with an AI coding agent can clone the visual layer of any app in an afternoon. The backend is the moat, for three compounding reasons:

1. **Integration switching cost.** Once Stripe, QuickBooks, email, and auth all flow through Connect, ripping it out means re-building and re-testing every integration — a project most small businesses will never prioritize.
2. **Data continuity.** Historical payment, usage, and churn data lives in our pipes. A competitor offering a prettier Scaffold has none of this history.
3. **Compounding Intelligence.** Every additional connected account makes the Intelligence layer's risk/churn signals more accurate, which makes the platform more valuable, which attracts more accounts. Classic data network effect — but only if Connect adoption is real, not hypothetical.

**The moat does not exist until Connect has real, paying, production usage.** Scaffold alone (open-source templates) has *no* moat — anyone can fork it. This is why Phase 1-2 prioritize getting Connect into production for real customers over polishing Scaffold.

## 7. Go-to-Market: Finding Your First 3 Customers

The original plan didn't specify *how* to find pilot users — this is the most commonly underestimated step. **All pilot recruitment happens in Phase 5** (after the entire platform is built in Phases 1-4). Concrete tactics:

1. **Warm network first.** List every person you know who owns or manages a small business in the med spa / wellness space — even loosely. Family, former colleagues, local community.
2. **Local Facebook/community groups.** Med spa owner and small-business-owner groups in your city are active and full of people complaining about exactly the tools you're replacing.
3. **Cold but specific outreach.** Find 20 local med spas via Google Maps. Send a short, specific message: *"I noticed you're using [X tool / no online booking]. I'm building a free portal for med spa businesses — would you be open to a 15-minute look?"* Specificity > volume.
4. **The pitch.**
   > *"I built a patient portal for med spa businesses. I'll set it up for you completely free for 90 days — no cost, no commitment. All I ask is 30 minutes of feedback every couple of weeks while I improve it."*

   This removes the only objection (cost) and reframes the ask as "feedback," which owners are usually happy to give.
5. **Target: 3 committed pilots in Phase 5.** The advantage of the build-first model is that by the time you contact clinics, the product is complete and polished — you're offering a finished platform, not a work-in-progress.
6. **Note on the 90-day promise.** Connect monetization doesn't start until months after onboarding — don't let this become a silent bait-and-switch: tell pilots upfront that the free period runs "through at least [Month 6]" rather than a hard 90-day cutoff, and treat the eventual conversion conversation as a planned step in the relationship (they're getting a longer free trial than promised, which is an easy goodwill point when the pricing conversation happens).

## 9. Decision Gates Summary (Kill Criteria)

Each phase has an explicit gate. If a gate is missed, **do not proceed to the next phase** — instead, diagnose:

| Gate | Threshold | If missed |
|---|---|---|
| Phase 0 → 1 | Pain points validated + pilot leads identified | Re-evaluate vertical choice (Section 6) before building anything |
| Phase 1 → 2 | All features built + tested + module gaps closed + RBAC complete + HIPAA resolved + staging smoke test passes | Continue building — close remaining gaps before generalizing |
| Phase 2 → 3 | Connect API functional + all modules generalized + repo open-source ready | Continue generalizing — modules must be truly vertical-agnostic |
| Phase 3 → 4 | Rules engine + marketplace + MCP server + ML scaffolding + home services portal all built | Core isn't reusable — refactor before launching |
| Phase 4 → 5 | Repo published + docs complete + MCP server published | Complete the open-source launch materials |
| Phase 5 → 6+ | 3+ pilots onboarded, 2+ using weekly, $500+ MRR, ML models trained | Iterate on customer feedback — the product needs refinement based on real usage |

This prevents the most common failure mode: delivering a half-finished product to early adopters and burning bridges. By building everything first with AI-accelerated development, customers get a polished, complete platform from day one.

## 10. Recommended Tech Stack

Chosen for AI-agent-friendliness (well-represented in training data, strong tooling, fast iteration):

- **Frontend:** Next.js (React) + Tailwind CSS — ubiquitous, AI agents generate it reliably
- **Backend/API:** Next.js API routes or a separate Node/Express service for Connect (separation matters once Connect becomes a standalone product)
- **Database:** Postgres (via Supabase or Neon) — relational integrity matters for payments/audit logs; Supabase also bundles auth primitives that can bootstrap `baseplate-core`
- **Payments:** Stripe (Connect's first and most important integration)
- **Email/SMS:** Postmark (email) + Twilio (SMS, add later if pilots need it)
- **Hosting:** Vercel (frontend) + Render/Fly.io (Connect API, since it needs to run independently of any one Scaffold deployment)
- **Auth:** Supabase Auth or Clerk — do not hand-roll auth; this is exactly the kind of "gnarly but solved" problem to delegate to a proven provider, then wrap it in `baseplate-core` for portability

### Repository Structure

Use a single monorepo (pnpm workspaces + Turborepo) from the start, even as a solo builder — this makes the Phase 2-3 extraction of shared code into `baseplate-core` and the standalone Connect API mechanical rather than a rewrite:

```
Med Spa App/                      # Monorepo root (also referenced as "baseplate/" in some docs)
  apps/
    portal-medspa/                # Med spa Scaffold template (Phase 1) — deployed to Vercel
    connect-api/                  # Layer 2 (built out starting Phase 2), deployed independently
  packages/
    core/                         # baseplate-core: auth wrapper, RBAC, audit log, encryption
    ui/                           # shared component library (buttons, tables, forms)
    patterns/                     # common workflow patterns (digital-signature, admin-setup)
  package.json
  turbo.json
  pnpm-workspace.yaml
```

`packages/core` and `packages/ui` are consumed by `apps/portal-[vertical]` from day one, even though there's only one vertical app — this is what makes Golden Rule #3 ("Modular over Monolith") concrete rather than aspirational. When the second vertical template is built in Phase 3, it becomes `apps/portal-[vertical-2]` consuming the same `packages/core`, proving the abstraction without a rewrite. `apps/connect-api` deploys to Render/Fly.io independently of the Vercel-hosted portal apps, since it needs to be reachable by external developers' Scaffold deployments in Phase 2+.

## 11. Your Golden Rules (Print This Out)

1. **You are the Architect, AI is the Bricklayer.** Never spend hours typing syntax. Spend hours thinking about system design, database structure, and user flow. Then tell Cursor/Claude to build it.
2. **B2B Only.** If an idea doesn't solve a business problem that makes money or saves time, drop it. No consumer apps. No social networks. No games.
3. **Modular over Monolith.** Every feature: *"Can I unplug this and use it in a completely different app tomorrow?"* If no, refactor it.
4. **Function over Form.** B2B buyers don't care about hover animations. They care that the invoice actually gets sent and the rent reminder actually fires.
5. **Don't skip the hard parts.** The money is in the boring, gnarly integrations (webhooks, auth edge cases, accounting sync). Anyone can build a dashboard; you are building the engine.
6. **Build Connect from demand, not speculation.** Every Connect endpoint should exist because a real user hit that wall — not because it seemed like a good idea.
7. **Depth before breadth.** One vertical, proven with paying customers, before a second vertical. One vertical's worth of Connect endpoints before a generic API surface. Real data before "AI-powered" claims.

## 13. Competitive Landscape

No single competitor occupies the "vertical Scaffold + opinionated Connect + AI-agent-native distribution" combination — but each piece individually has incumbents worth knowing:

| Category | Examples | Why they don't own this space |
|---|---|---|
| **Vertical SaaS incumbents** | Mangomint, Boulevard, Vagaro, Mindbody (med spas) | Mature, feature-rich, but closed platforms — not open-source, not AI-agent-friendly, and expensive for small operators ($100-400+/mo) |
| **Generic AI app builders** | Lovable, v0, Bolt, Replit Agent | Excellent at generating UI/app code quickly, but produce one-off apps with no integration layer, no vertical domain knowledge baked in, and no ongoing relationship with the business |
| **Open-source SaaS boilerplates** | ShipFast, SaaS Pegasus, Supabase starter templates | Generic (auth + billing + landing page) — not vertical-specific, and have no equivalent to the Connect middleware |
| **Integration platforms** | Zapier, Make, Merge.dev, Unified.to | Merge.dev and Unified.to are the closest existing analogs to "Connect" (unified APIs over multiple providers), but they target enterprise engineering teams integrating their *own* product — not AI agents scaffolding a *new* vertical app from a template |

**The wedge:** none of these combine all three — a vertical-specific open-source Scaffold, a pre-wired opinionated integration layer (Connect), and AI-agent-native distribution (so AI coding tools default to using it). Baseplate's bet is that owning all three together, for one vertical first, creates a combination none of the above can easily replicate without becoming a different company.

### Competitive Urgency

This table reflects *today's* landscape, not a static one. If "vertical Scaffold + opinionated Connect" is the right wedge, it's also legible to well-funded teams — a16z/YC-style "Stripe for AI-built vertical SaaS" pitches are a predictable next step in this space. The defense against this isn't a feature or a patent; it's **speed to real customer lock-in**. The entire Phase 1-2 timeline (Section 8) is designed to get Connect into production for paying customers in one vertical *before* a better-funded competitor notices the niche is worth targeting. If a well-capitalized competitor appears before Phase 2's exit gate, do not try to out-build them — narrow further (a sub-niche within the chosen vertical) rather than compete head-on for the broad wedge.

## 15. Legal & Compliance Checklist

These items are easy to skip early but become expensive to retrofit once pilot users' real customer/tenant/patient data is flowing through the system.

- **Business entity (before Phase 5 pilots go live).** Operate under a simple LLC rather than as an individual once real third-party data (tenant info, payment details, patient contact info) is being processed — even for free pilots. This limits personal liability and is required by most payment processors for a business Stripe account.
- **Terms of Service + Privacy Policy (before Phase 5 pilots go live).** Use a generator (Termly, GetTerms) for a baseline ToS/Privacy Policy covering data handling, since pilots are handling real end-customer data (tenants, patients, clients) through the portal.
- **Stripe account under the business entity**, not a personal account — required for Connect endpoints to eventually process real transaction volume.
- **Vertical-specific considerations:**
  - **Med Spas (chosen vertical):** Do not store Protected Health Information (medical history, treatment notes, photos) in the portal without a BAA in place with infrastructure providers (Supabase, Vercel). Scope strictly to scheduling, contact info, and payment — this keeps the system outside HIPAA's PHI definition until HIPAA-eligible infrastructure is in place. Any future health-data features require HIPAA-eligible infrastructure (e.g., AWS BAA-covered services, Supabase Pro with BAA) before being built. **Note:** The intake forms capture medical history and consent — execute a BAA with Supabase before Phase 5 pilot launch, or restrict intake to non-PHI fields only. This is resolved during Phase 1 build completion.
- **Data Processing Addendum (Phase 5+).** Once Connect is processing real third-party data for paying customers, add a basic DPA to the Connect terms — most B2B buyers will eventually ask for one.
